\subsection{N-Assets Optimal Portfolio}\label{nassetssec}
    \textit{Here I will give a mathematical description of the optimisation problem presented by Markowitz, including some short proofs of the key points used to formulate the optimal solution as the solution of a linear equation system.}
    
    \begin{definition}[Optimal Mean-Variance Portfolio \cite{markowitzorg}]
        Let $N \in \mathbb{N}_{\geq 0}$ be the number of assets considered for the portfolio construction. Then the vector with the allocation weights for each of the $N$ assets in the \textbf{optimal portfolio for a given level of return $\mu_P \in \mathbb{R}$} is given by the solution of the following optimisation problem
        \begin{align*}
            \arg \min_{w \in \mathbb{R}^N}& \quad w^T \cdot \Sigma \cdot w \\
            \text{s.t.}& \quad \mathbf{1}^T \cdot w = 1 \\
            & \quad \mu^T \cdot w = \mu_P
        \end{align*}
        
        where $\mathbf{1} := [ 1, \dots, 1]^T \in \mathbb{R}^N$, $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ is the covariance matrix of returns and $\mu \in \mathbb{R}^N$ is the vector of expected returns of the $N$ assets.
        
        The set of pairs $(\mu_p, \sigma_P) \in \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ such that, for each $\mu_P \in \mathbb{R}$, $\sigma_P := w_{opt}^T \cdot \Sigma \cdot w_{opt}$ where $w_{opt}$ is the solution to the optimisation problem above is called the \textbf{efficient frontier}.
    \end{definition}
    
    The main critique point of this optimisation problem is the high sensitivity to small changes in the expected returns vector, which leads to significantly different portfolio allocations even for a unchanged covariance matrix \cite{michaudreturnsstability}. Because returns are difficult to forecast accurately enough to keep the sensitivity within certain limits, some asset managers abstain from using them as input parameter. Portfolio allocations are then computed on the basis of the covariance matrix only, which is a risk parameter, hence the name \textit{risk-based} asset allocation. In the case of Markowitz's portfolio optimisation model, risk-based optimal portfolios correspond to minimum volatility portfolios.
    
    \begin{definition}[Minimum Volatility Portfolio \cite{berndscherer}]
        Let $N \in \mathbb{N}_{\geq 0}$ be the number of assets considered for the portfolio construction. Then the vector with the allocation weights for each of the $N$ assets in the \textbf{minimum volatility portfolio} is given by the solution of the following optimisation problem
        \begin{align*}
            \arg \min_{w \in \mathbb{R}^N}& \quad w^T \cdot \Sigma \cdot w \\
            \text{s.t.}& \quad \mathbf{1}^T \cdot w = 1
        \end{align*}
    \end{definition}

    \begin{theorem}
        Let the covariance matrix $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ be given. Assuming that $\Sigma$ is non-singular, the minimum volatility portfolio allocation vector is given by
        \begin{align*}
            w_{opt} := \frac{1}{\mathbf{1}^T \cdot \Sigma^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{1}} \cdot \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}
        \end{align*}
    \end{theorem}
    
    \begin{proof}
        Let $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ be a non-singular covariance matrix. We define the Lagrangian corresponding to the minimum volatility portfolio optimisation problem as follows
        \begin{align*}
            \mathcal{L}(w, \lambda) := w^T \cdot \Sigma \cdot w - \lambda (\mathbf{1}^T \cdot w - 1)
        \end{align*}
        
        To find the solution to the optimisation problem, we take the derivatives with respect to both $w$ and $\lambda$, and set them to $0$.
        \begin{align*}
            \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w} &= 2 \cdot \Sigma \cdot w - \lambda \cdot \mathbf{1} \\
            \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \lambda} &= 1 - \mathbf{1}^T \cdot w
        \end{align*}
        \begin{align*}
            \Rightarrow \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w} = 0 & \Leftrightarrow & 2 \cdot \Sigma \cdot w &= \lambda \cdot \mathbf{1} \\
            & \Leftrightarrow & w &= \Sigma^{-1} \cdot \left( \frac{\lambda}{2} \cdot \mathbf{1} \right) \\
            & \Leftrightarrow & w &= \frac{\lambda}{2} \cdot \Sigma^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{1}
        \end{align*}\todo{better format alignment of left arrows}
        \begin{align*}
            \Rightarrow \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \lambda} = 0 & \Leftrightarrow & 1 - \mathbf{1}^T \cdot w &= 0 \\
            & \Leftrightarrow & 1 - \mathbf{1}^T \cdot \frac{\lambda}{2} \cdot \Sigma^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{1} &= 0 \\
            & \Leftrightarrow & 1 - \frac{\lambda}{2} \cdot \mathbf{1}^T \cdot \Sigma^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{1} &= 0 \\
            & \Leftrightarrow & \frac{\lambda}{2} \cdot \mathbf{1}^T \cdot \Sigma^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{1} &= 1 \\
            & \Leftrightarrow & \frac{\lambda}{2} &= \frac{1}{\mathbf{1}^T \cdot \Sigma^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{1}} \\
            & \Leftrightarrow & \lambda &= \frac{2}{\mathbf{1}^T \cdot \Sigma^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{1}}
        \end{align*}\todo{better format alignment of left arrows}
        \begin{align*}
            w_{opt} := \frac{\frac{2}{\mathbf{1}^T \cdot \Sigma^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{1}}}{2} \cdot \Sigma^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{1} = \frac{1}{\mathbf{1}^T \cdot \Sigma^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{1}} \cdot \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}
        \end{align*}
    \end{proof}
    
    The analytical solution presented above relies on computing the inverse of the covariance matrix used as input. Because covariance matrices are only positive-semidefinite, i.e. not necessarily non-singular, and because computing the inverse of a matrix is computationally very expensive, we would like to redefine the analytical solution as the solution of a linear system. However, we will further assume that the covariance matrix used is non-singular.
    
    \begin{theorem}\label{markowitzlinearsystem}
         Let the covariance matrix $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ be given. Assuming that $\Sigma$ is non-singular, the minimum volatility portfolio allocation vector can be computed as follows:
         \begin{enumerate}
             \item Solve the linear system $\Sigma \cdot w = \mathbf{1}$, and define its solution as $\Tilde{w}_{opt} \in \mathbb{R}^T$. (Note that if $\Sigma$ is non-singular, $w_{opt}$ might not be unique or even exist).
             \item Normalise $\Tilde{w}_{opt}$ for its elements to sum up to $1$, i.e. set $w_{opt} = \frac{1}{\mathbf{1}^T \cdot \Tilde{w}_{opt}} \cdot \Tilde{w}_{opt}$.
         \end{enumerate}
    \end{theorem}
    
    \begin{proof}
        Let the covariance matrix $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ be given and non-singular. Moreover, let $\Tilde{w}_{opt}$ be the solution of the linear system $\Sigma \cdot w = \mathbf{1}$. Then we can write $\Tilde{w}_{opt} = \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1}$.
        \begin{align*}
            \Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{\mathbf{1}^T \cdot \Tilde{w}_{opt}} = \frac{1}{\mathbf{1}^T \cdot \Sigma^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{1}} \cdot \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{1} = w_{opt}
        \end{align*}
    \end{proof}

\subsection{Practical Implementation}
    %\textit{In this section, I will describe the optimal portfolios computed within the practical implementation framework}
    
    For the practical implementation, we use Numpy's linear system solver, which computes a solution to the linear system in Theorem \ref{markowitzlinearsystem} using LAPACK routine \_gesv. This means, that the solution is computed using the $LU$ decomposition of the covariance matrix with partial pivoting.
    
    \begin{theorem}[LU-Decomposition with Partial Pivoting \cite{mirsky}]
        Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ be a non-singular matrix, then there exists a unique factorisation
        \begin{align*}
            A := P \cdot L \cdot U
        \end{align*}
        where $P \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ is a permutation matrix, $L \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ is a unit lower triangular matrix and $U \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ is an upper triangular matrix.
    \end{theorem}
