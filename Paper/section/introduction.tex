% - Why is asset allocation important? Short description of the asset allocation optimisation problem.
% - Short history of Markowitz's Modern Portfolio Theory.

How to allocate the limited resources across the set of investable assets available. This is the challenge all investors are faced with, because no investor has unlimited capital available to invest. This task gets more complicated when investors have to take into account their risk appetite and their target return. This asset allocation problem can be defined as an optimisation problem with 2 variations:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Maximise returns while maintaining risk within a given risk tolerance level, or
    \item Minimise risk for a given level of target return.
\end{enumerate}

Harry M. Markowitz was the first to develop and present a systematic approach to this asset allocation problem in both variations in 1952, by defining risk as the standard deviation of past returns and returns as the expected value of past returns. His work earned him a Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences in 1990. Markowitz's portfolio construction model laid the ground for quantitative asset allocation models. Many asset allocation models today are developed using Markowitz's model and adjusting it to account for modern investors' needs. For example, by using risk measures other than the standard deviation of past returns, or by targeting functions other than expected returns.

The main critique point of Markowitz's portfolio construction model is the lack of stability that leads to a high portfolio turn-over and to concentrated portfolios, specially when correlations rise. In this paper, we analyse Markowitz's portfolio construction model and the causes of low stability by focusing on the minimum volatility portfolio. Moreover, we present some techniques that try to improve the stability of minimum volatility portfolios: 1 regarding parameter estimation for Markowitz's model and 1 alternative allocation method. Moreover, we also compare Markowitz's minimum volatility portfolios to 2 naive allocation methods: equal weight and inverse variance allocation.

    \subsubsection{Backtest Settings}
        In total, 12 portfolios are computed, corresponding to a backtesting period from January 1, 2020 to December 31, 2020 and monthly rebalancing-interval. The rebalancing occurs on the first trading day of each month at 10:00 AM New York time. An initial account balance of 1 million U.S. dollars is assumed, and margin calls are deactivated in order to better simulate the theoretical portfolios computed.
        
        For the computation of portfolio weights, the daily returns of the past 60 days is used as input. This last setting does not affect the equal weight portfolio construction method, because this naive method does not use any past data as input. The reason why we use 60 days of past data is the following.
        
        Let $N \in \mathbb{N} \setminus \{ 0 \}$ be the number of assets considered for the portfolio construction and $T \in \mathbb{N} \setminus \{ 0 \}$ the number of past periods data used as input for the optimisation model. Because the models analysed here rely upon the computation of a covariance matrix (except for the 2 naive methods: equal weight and inverse variance allocation), we need to compute one. Let the matrix that contains the $T$ returns in each row for all $N$ assets be defined as $R \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times T}$. The, $rank(R) \leq \min \{ N, T \}$ holds.
        
        Let now $\Sigma := [\sigma_{i,j}]_{i,j = 1, \dots, N} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ be the covariance matrix of the $N$ assets, where $\sigma_{i,j}$ represents the covariance of the $i$th and $j$th asset, then $\Sigma$ can be computed as
        \begin{align*}
            \Sigma = \frac{1}{T} (\Tilde{R}^T \cdot \Tilde{R})
        \end{align*}
        where $\Tilde{R} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times T}$ is defined as the matrix $R$ after centring the data row-wise, i.e. after subtracting from each element in each row the expected value of that same row. From this definition of $\Sigma$, we get that
        \begin{align*}
            rank(\Sigma) \leq rank(\Tilde{R}) \leq rank(R) \leq \min \{ N, T \}
        \end{align*}
        
        That means that $\Sigma \in GL_N(\mathbb{R})$ if only if $rank(\Sigma) = rank(\Tilde{R}) = rank(R) = N$, i.e. $N \leq T$ must hold.
        
        In our practical implementation framework, we have selected $N = 20$ assets, therefore $T \geq 20$ must hold. We chose $T = 60$ because it corresponds to 3 full trading months of data.
    
    \subsubsection{Portfolio Analyses}
        
        % Tools to analyse PFs: HHI for concentration, risk-adjusted returns, stability: turn-over volume, what else?
        
        The portfolios generated by each optimisation method in backtesting will be analysed with respect to risk-adjusted return, concentration and stability. The Sharpe ratio is the chosen measure to quantify risk-returns. To assess concentration, we will use a normalised version of the Herfindahl-Hirsch-Index (HHI). The normalisation step is necessary, because we will allow for short positions in our portfolios, and the HHI is only defined for positive portfolio weights. We will measure stability via the turnover of the portfolio.
        
        \begin{definition}[Sharpe Ratio \cite{franzen}] % Page 216
            Let $\mu_P \in \mathbb{R}$ be the return of investment $P$ and $\sigma_P \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ its standard deviation. Moreover, let $R_f \in \mathbb{R}$ be the risk-free rate. Then the \textbf{Sharpe Ratio of investment $P$} $SR_P \in \mathbb{R}$ is given by
            \begin{align*}
                SR_P := \frac{\mu_P - R_f}{\sigma_P}
            \end{align*}
        \end{definition}
        
        \begin{remark}
            In this paper, we do not compute the Sharpe ratio of the backtest separately, and rely upon QuantConnect's internal overall portfolio statistics for its computation, i.e. all Sharpe ratios presented in this paper are the Sharpe ratios that appear in QuantConnect's backtesting results. QuantConnect computes the Sharpe ratio using the same definition as above, but using the annualised return of the portfolio in the backtest \cite{qcsharperatiocode}.
        \end{remark}

        \begin{definition}[Herfindahl-Hirsch-Index \cite{chammas}] % Page 71 (85 of the PDF)
            Let $P$ be a portfolio containing $N$ assets with weights $w_i \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ for all $i=1, \dots, N$, such that $\sum_{i=1}^N w_i = 1$. We define the \textbf{Herfindahl-Hirsch-Index (HHI)} as the sum of the squares of the weights, i.e.
            \begin{align*}
                HHI_P := \sum_{i=1}^N w_i^2
            \end{align*}
        \end{definition}
        
        \begin{definition}[Normalised Herfindahl-Hirsch-Index]
            Let $P$ be a portfolio containing $N$ assets with weights $w_i \in \mathbb{R}$ for all $i=1, \dots, N$, such that $\sum_{i=1}^N w_i = 1$. We define the \textbf{normalised Herfindahl-Hirsch-Index (nHHI)} as the sum of the squares of the normalised weights, i.e.
            \begin{align*}
                nHHI_P := \sum_{i=1}^N \left( \frac{|w_i|}{\sum_{j=1}^N |w_j|}\right)^2
            \end{align*}
        \end{definition}
        
        \begin{remark}%\todo{add reference to QuantExchange?} % https://quant.stackexchange.com/questions/65525/herfindahl-hirsch-index-for-fx-portfolios?noredirect=1#comment92677_65525
            Let $P$ be a portfolio containing $N$ assets with weights $w_i \in \mathbb{R}$ for all $i=1, \dots, N$, such that $\sum_{i=1}^N w_i = 1$. Because $\sum_{i=1}^N |w_i| \geq \left| \sum_{i=1}^N w_i \right|$, the following holds
            \begin{align*}
                nHHI_P &= \sum_{i=1}^N \left( \frac{|w_i|}{\sum_{j=1}^N |w_j|}\right)^2 \\
                &= \left( \frac{1}{\sum_{j=1}^N |w_j|}\right)^2 \sum_{i=1}^N w_i^2 \\
                &= \left( \frac{1}{\sum_{j=1}^N |w_j|}\right)^2 HHI_P \leq HHI_P
            \end{align*}
            i.e. for a given portfolio $P$, the Herfindahl-Hirsch-Index is smaller or equal than the normalised Herfindahl-Hirsch-Index.
        \end{remark}
        
        \begin{definition}[Turnover \cite{equalweight}]
            Consider a portfolio $P$ with $N$ assets. Let $w_{j,t+1} \in \mathbb{R}$ be the portfolio weight assigned to asset $i$ at time $t$ by the portfolio optimisation method, and let $w_{j,t^+} \in \mathbb{R}$ be the portfolio weight of asset $i$ before rebalancing at time $t+1$ for $i=1, \dots, N$ and $t=0, \dots, T$. The turnover of the portfolio $P$ is defined as
            \begin{align*}
                TO_P := \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=0}^T \sum_{i=1}^N \left( \left| w_{i, t+1} - w_{i, t^+} \right| \right)
            \end{align*}
            where $T \in \mathbb{N}$ is the number of rebalancing points of the portfolio.
            
            This turnover quantity can be interpreted as the average percentage of wealth traded at each rebalancing point.
        \end{definition}
        
    \subsubsection{Benchmark Portfolio Construction Methods}
        We use 2 naive portfolio allocation models, equal weight and inverse variance allocation, as benchmarks for the other 3 methods (Markowitz minimum volatility portfolio, Markowitz minimum volatility portfolio with covariance matrix shrinking and hierarchical risk parity) to estimate if the the increased complexity of the latter can be justified in form of better risk-adjusted returns, concentration and stability.
        
        \begin{definition}[Equal Weight Portfolio \cite{equalweight}]
            Consider a portfolio of $N \in \mathbb{N}_{>0}$ assets, and let $w \in \mathbb{R}^N$ be the vector of allocation weights. The \textbf{equal weight allocation portfolio (EWP)} is defined as
            \begin{align*}
                w_i := \frac{1}{N} \quad \text{for all} \quad i = 1, \dots, N
            \end{align*}
        \end{definition}
        
        The equal weight portfolio is the most widely used allocation method when no information about the assets in the portfolio is available to rank them or compute some measure to define some other portfolio. It is also the most naive way of allocating capital across the $N$ assets considered. However, this naive method has proven to perform even better than other, even very sophisticated, methods \cite{equalweight}.
        
        \begin{definition}[Inverse Variance Portfolio \cite{equalweight}]
            Consider a portfolio of $N \in \mathbb{N}_{>0}$ assets, and let $w \in \mathbb{R}^N$ be the vector of allocation weights. The \textbf{inverse variance allocation portfolio (IVP)} is defined as
            \begin{align*}
                w_i := \frac{\frac{1}{\mathbb{V}[X_i]}}{\sum_{k=1}^N \mathbb{V}[X_k]} \quad \text{for} \quad i=1, \dots, N
            \end{align*}
            where $\mathbb{V}[X_i]$ is the variance of the returns' distribution of the $i$-th asset.
        \end{definition}
        
        \begin{remark}
            The inverse variance allocation portfolio is the solution of the following optimisation problem
            \begin{align*}
                \arg \min_{w \in \mathbb{R}^N}& \quad w^T \cdot \Sigma \cdot w \\
                \text{s.t.}& \quad \mathbf{1}^T \cdot w = 1
            \end{align*}
            where $\Sigma := diag( \mathbb{V}[X_1], \dots, \mathbb{V}[X_N] )$ such that $X_i$ is the returns distribution of the $i$-th asset.
            
            This means that the inverse variance portfolio is the minimum volatility portfolio considering all assets uncorrelated.
        \end{remark}
        
        Although IVP is more complex than the equal weight allocation  model because the estimation of some parameters is needed, it is still considered a naive method.
